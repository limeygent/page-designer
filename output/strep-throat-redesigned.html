Is It Strep Throat or a Viral Sore Throat?

Strep throat is a bacterial infection that requires antibiotics. Most sore throats are caused by viruses and resolve on their own. Here is how to tell the difference.

Strep Throat (Bacterial)

  • Onset: Sudden, often overnight
  • Fever: 101°F or higher, common
  • Cough: Absent (key differentiator)
  • Tonsils: Red, swollen, white patches or streaks
  • Runny Nose: Absent
  • Stomach Pain: Common in children under 10
  • Peak Season: November through March (North TX)
  • Treatment: Requires antibiotics (10-day course)

Viral Sore Throat

  • Onset: Gradual, over 1 to 2 days
  • Fever: Low-grade or no fever
  • Cough: Usually present
  • Tonsils: May be red, rarely have patches
  • Runny Nose: Common
  • Stomach Pain: Uncommon
  • Peak Season: Year-round
  • Treatment: Rest, fluids, symptom relief only

A rapid strep test is the only way to confirm strep throat. Visual inspection alone is not reliable. If your child has 2 or more strep symptoms above, call us for a same-day visit.

  • Severe neck swelling or stiffness

What to Know About Strep Throat Treatment

Once your child is diagnosed, here is what the treatment and recovery process looks like.

The standard treatment is amoxicillin taken twice daily for 10 full days. It is critical to finish the entire course even after symptoms improve (usually within 48 hours). Stopping early increases the risk of the infection returning and developing antibiotic resistance. If your child has a penicillin allergy, we prescribe an alternative such as azithromycin or a cephalosporin.

Most children start feeling noticeably better within 24 to 48 hours of starting antibiotics. Fever typically resolves within 24 hours. Sore throat pain decreases significantly by day 2 to 3. Full recovery takes about a week. If your child is not improving after 48 hours on antibiotics, call us for a follow-up (telemedicine available).

While antibiotics work, help your child feel comfortable: offer cold fluids, popsicles, or warm broth. Ibuprofen or acetaminophen can reduce fever and throat pain (follow age-appropriate dosing). Use a cool-mist humidifier at night. Soft foods like yogurt and applesauce are easier to swallow. Avoid acidic or spicy foods.

Strep is highly contagious through respiratory droplets and shared items. Replace your child's toothbrush after 24 hours on antibiotics. Do not share cups, utensils, or towels. Encourage frequent handwashing. Siblings with sore throat symptoms within 2 to 5 days should be tested. We can test multiple children during a single visit.

Why Strep Throat Peaks in Frisco Every Winter

November Through March

Strep cases surge when children spend more time indoors. Frisco ISD schools see clusters spreading through classrooms. When one child in Phillips Creek Ranch or Richwoods is diagnosed, siblings and classmates frequently follow within days.

Cedar Season Overlap

Cedar fever (December through February) causes sore throats and fatigue that mimic viral infections. A rapid strep test removes the guesswork in 10 minutes.

Daycare and School Spread

Frisco's communities have a high density of daycares and elementary schools. Group A Streptococcus spreads quickly through shared toys, sneezing, and close contact. Ages 5 to 15 are most susceptible.

Common Questions About Strep Throat in Children

Our in-office rapid strep test delivers results in approximately 10 minutes. The throat swab itself takes just a few seconds. If the rapid test is negative but clinical suspicion is high, we send a backup throat culture to the lab, with results available in 24 to 48 hours.

Amoxicillin is the first-line treatment, taken twice daily for 10 days. For children with a penicillin allergy, alternatives include azithromycin (5-day course) or a cephalosporin. We only prescribe antibiotics when the strep test is positive.

Under Frisco ISD's illness policy, your child may return to school after being on antibiotics for at least 24 hours AND being fever-free for 24 hours without fever-reducing medication. For example, a child diagnosed Monday morning can typically return Wednesday. We provide absence documentation at your visit.

Strep throat requires an in-office rapid strep test for accurate diagnosis, so the initial visit must be in person. However, we offer telemedicine follow-ups if your child is not improving after 48 hours on antibiotics, or if you have questions about the treatment plan.

Recurrent strep throat is defined as 7 or more episodes in one year, 5 or more per year for two consecutive years, or 3 or more per year for three consecutive years. If your child meets these thresholds, we discuss referral to a pediatric ENT specialist for evaluation.

Untreated strep can lead to serious complications: rheumatic fever (which can damage heart valves permanently), post-streptococcal glomerulonephritis (kidney inflammation), peritonsillar abscess, and scarlet fever. These complications are rare when strep is treated promptly with antibiotics.
